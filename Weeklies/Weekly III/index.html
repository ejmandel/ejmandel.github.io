Eddie Mandell ~ Weekly 3

How is materiality manifested in poor images?

Hito Steyerl in the chapter “In Defense of the Poor Image” of the book The Wretched of the Screen explains the “poor image,” or the low resolution rendition of what once existed at a much richer quality. From his writing, we can glean that Steyerl sees that poor images essentially represent the easy spread of the digital image, specifically at a lower, “poorer” resolution, “images that can be made and seen by many” (Steyerl 41). In this sense, the low res image has become a commodity in that it is cheaply processed and distributed at mass volume. An example of the poor image at work in the cultural sphere lies in the notion of bootlegging. By pirating or ripping media, either visual or aural, from its original source and recoding it, one creates a poor image that allows a larger audience to experience the bootlegged piece. In my opinion, though this may be a bit of a stretch, another good example of this concept is the knock-off merchandise sold outside of concert venues. Though this doesn’t necessarily have to do with motion-picture art, in a way this is a poor image of an artists design and branding on official merchandise that has been reproduced at lower quality with lower skill to be purchased for less money by a willing crowd.
